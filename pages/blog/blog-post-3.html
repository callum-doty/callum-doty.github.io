Empathetic Salmon

Improbable things happen more often than you'd think.

Published on May 30, 2025

Imagine I told you a dead salmon can correctly assess the emotions of humans. You would probably be pretty skeptical or wave me off thinking I had gone a bit crazy. I would counter with concrete evidence of fMRI images of a salmon's brain in which each scan, broken down into voxels (an fMRI method that breaks each scan down into thousands of pieces), corresponds to small sections of the salmon brain. Two groups of these voxels — one in the spinal column and the other in the medial brain cavity — were highly successful at recognizing human facial expressions.

Woah! Imagine how empathetic a living salmon would be!

This was an actual study presented at the 2009 Organization for Human Brain Mapping Conference (it won an Ig Nobel prize).

The reality is that there is a random amount of noise per voxel. Given that a salmon nervous system contains thousands of voxels, the odds of a voxel randomly spiking is high. What are the chances the noise spiked when a salmon was shown an image of a person crying? High enough to occur consecutive times in two regions of the salmon brain! The beauty of this study is the fact that improbable things happen all the time and that there are flaws in scientific research.

Applying this to everyday life, it is not hard to find a self-help social media post claiming a new solution to weight loss. Claiming that eliminating all carbohydrates is good for you and has helped numerous people. Similar to the voxel spikes, thousands of viewers bought into this idea and tried it. The odds are, by chance alone, someone lost weight. Those few people spread news of their success and get the most attention.

Let's put this in an equation representing the odds of weight loss given solution x. Assumptions:

  • Each person's outcome is independent of each other
  • The probability p of losing weight due to the solution or by random chance is the same per person
  • The outcome for each person is binary: they either lose weight (success) or they do not (failure)
  • There is a sufficiently large number of people n trying the solution

Variables:

  • n — total number of people trying the weight loss solution
  • p — probability of weight loss due to the solution or random chance
  • k — number of people who report successful weight loss

To calculate the probability of exactly k people losing weight out of n people trying, we use the binomial distribution. Imagine 5 people trying this diet — 3 out of 5 report success:

P(X = k) = C(n, k) × pk × (1−p)n−k

Applying to our weight loss example:

  • n = 5 (total people)
  • k = 3 (successful people)
  • p = 0.09 (9% chance of random success)

Breaking down the equation:

  • C(5,3) = 10 ways to pick 3 from 5
  • (0.09)3 = 0.000729 — probability of 3 successes
  • (0.91)2 = 0.8281 — probability of 2 failures

10 × 0.000729 × 0.8281 ≈ 0.0060 (0.6%)

There's about a 0.6% chance of seeing exactly 3 successful weight loss stories out of 5 people, just by random chance. Scale that to thousands of viewers and improbable outcomes become inevitable — kind of like finding an empathetic salmon.
