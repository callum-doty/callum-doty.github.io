Chess to Problems

Published on July 4, 2026

I believe that people reveal more through repeated decisions than through questionnaires.

Ask someone if they're risk tolerant, patient, or analytical and you'll probably get the version of themselves they aspire to be. Give them a constrained environment where they have to make hundreds of decisions under pressure, and you'll get something more honest.

For me, that environment is chess.

After enough games, your opening repertoire stops being a collection of memorized moves and starts becoming a style of problem solving. Every game begins with a choice. Do you want initiative or long-term pressure? Stability or imbalance? Do you simplify the position or intentionally make it more complicated?

The opening itself isn't the interesting part. What is interesting is why you keep choosing it.

To visualize the relationship in my head: Chess openings lead to observable patterns which lead to a decision architecture and you can map that architecture to other domains.

That middle step is important. Openings don't define the entire problem solving approach. They expose stable preferences under a common set of constraints.

Dimensions

Instead of thinking about openings individually, I think it's more useful to think about the dimensions they reveal.

Chess Preference What It May Reveal
Tactical vs Positional Immediate opportunity vs delayed value
Sharp vs Solid Risk tolerance
Open vs Closed Comfort with complexity
Initiative vs Material Short-term momentum vs accumulated advantage
Theory-heavy vs Flexible Preparation vs adaptation
Simplification vs Complication Error minimization vs upside seeking

No single game tells you much, but hundreds of games do.

My Own Playstyle

If I had to summarize my own chess, I'd call myself an asymmetrical positional player.

That sounds contradictory at first. When people hear positional, they often imagine slow, quiet chess where tiny advantages accumulate over dozens of moves.

That's not it.

I enjoy open positions. I like giving both players opportunities because I think complicated positions create more room for superior decision making and room for error. Rather than reducing uncertainty, I prefer environments where both sides have many candidate moves, many strategic ideas, and plenty of opportunities to make mistakes.

I want difficult positions where the quality of the decision matters more than memorized theory.

I'm rarely interested in sacrificing material simply because an engine says the attack works. Instead, I try to build positions where every tactical opportunity grows naturally from long-term strategic advantages. The tactics are a consequence of the position, not the objective.

That distinction probably says more about how I think than any individual opening ever could.

My prefered openings are the english/catalan for white and the sicilian/indian defense for black.

The English (1. c4): By controlling the center from the flank rather than occupying it immediately with 1. d4 or 1. e4, you immediately create an asymmetrical pawn structure. The English rarely forces immediate tactical clashes. Instead, it creates highly complex, fluid middlegames where both sides have a massive web of candidate moves. It is the ultimate “preserve optionality” opening for White.

The Catalan: This is the epitome of long-term positional pressure. By fianchettoing the light-squared bishop, you are investing in a long-term strategic asset that radiates pressure across the board. The tactics in the Catalan almost always arise organically because Black's position eventually cracks under the geometric pressure of that bishop and White's central control. It perfectly fits your goal of accumulating strategic advantages that naturally bloom into tactical opportunities.

The Sicilian Defense (1… c5): Against 1. e4, responding symmetrically with 1… e5 often allows White to dictate the pace or force simplifications. By playing 1… c5, you immediately unbalance the board. You are telling White, “I am willing to give you a space advantage or attacking chances on the kingside in exchange for a central pawn majority and long-term structural leverage.” It guarantees a complicated game where both sides have opportunities to blunder.

Indian Defenses (1. d4 Nf6): Whether you play the Nimzo-Indian, King's Indian, or Grünfeld, the core philosophy is hypermodernism: let White build a big center, then attack it. This requires a high comfort level with complexity and delayed gratification. You are intentionally inviting a complex problem to solve, relying on your ability to navigate the resulting imbalances better than your opponent.

Beyond Chess

This is where I think the idea becomes interesting.

I notice myself solving problems the same way outside of chess.

When I'm building machine learning models, I build a simple model first, but I don't usually search for the simplest possible solution. I look for representations that preserve optionality. I want systems that expose multiple paths forward rather than collapsing onto one answer.

When I think about investing or prediction markets, I'm less interested in finding certainty than I am in identifying situations where complexity has been mispriced. If everyone understands the obvious line, I'd rather spend time understanding the unusual one.

Even when I'm writing software, I gravitate toward architectures that are flexible enough to evolve instead of being perfectly optimized for today's requirements.

In a nutshell: preserve optionality, embrace complexity when it creates leverage, and let better decisions separate themselves over time.
